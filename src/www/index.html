A Brief History

The concept of extraversion and introversion is probably the oldest beliefs in the history of personality theory as it dates back to the 1920s with the psychologist Carl Jung. He believes there are two mutually exclusive attitudes: extraversion and introversion – “each person seems to be energized by either the external world (extraversion) or the internal world (introversion).” Extraversion and introversion are typically observed as a single continuum, so to be high in one necessitates being low in the other – which suggests that everyone has both an extraverted side and an introverted side.


Are You an Introvert?

You enjoy spending quality time alone and would rather have a quiet intimate conversation with someone than a loud group conversation. You are someone who dreads situations where you must interact with others for a long period of time, and you listen to music in public mostly so that you can avoid any sorts of interaction. People who are introverted tend to be more focused on internal thoughts, feelings and moods rather than seeking out external simulation.

Acceptance

According to estimates, extraverts outnumber introverts by around three to one. Extraverts usually have a hard time understanding introverts - they simply cannot imagine why someone would need to be alone. Often, extraverts try to change introverts or even suggest that there may be something "wrong" with them - though that is far from the truth. While introverts make up a smaller portion of the population, there is no right or wrong personality type. Instead, both introverts and extraverts should try to understand and accept each other's differences and similarities.
